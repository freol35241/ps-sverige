## Editorial pivot: voter-actionable framing

Reviewing the long-form piece, the criticism was that it reads
interesting but un-actionable for a voter. The findings are
descriptive; the voter has to do all the translation themselves.

Two changes to fix this without turning the piece into a voting guide:

1. **"So what" sentences after each major finding.** Pattern → vote
   consequence, in one paragraph. Added five:
   - After the behavioural map: C is behaviourally on the opposition
     side of the bloc line, not the centre.
   - After the three vote-vs-reason pairs: M and SD are not two
     separate centre-right options on the record; V and MP are the
     closest thing in the chamber to a single party.
   - After SD's co-authorship gap: SD's vote count and signature
     count tell different stories.
   - After the mechanism vocabulary drift: vocabulary shifts are
     early signals of where each party's centre of gravity is moving.
   - After the Tidö collapse: a vote for any of the four Tidö parties
     in 2026 is, on the record of this mandate, a vote for the same
     package.

2. **Issue-routing guide.** Replaced the abstract "Where does your
   priority issue live?" closing subsection with a concrete topic
   table: migration / international aid / climate / welfare-expansion
   topics each get a one-line cluster-pattern reading, plus a catch-all
   line for non-polarising topics.

What I rejected: per-party verdicts (option 2 in the discussion).
Maximally useful at the ballot box but shifts the piece from
observational to evaluative; not willing to defend eight verdicts
individually right now.

Open question: whether the inline "so what" framing ("at the ballot
box", "read as a forecast") starts to feel repetitive across the
piece. Five insertions in a long article — probably fine, but worth
checking with a fresh read.

## Structural rewrite: Alternative C and rename to "Riksdag Revealed"

The voter-actionable patches above improved the piece locally but the
structure remained incoherent. The "four prior questions" scaffold
(diagnosis / end state / mechanism / values) didn't carry the strongest
findings — bloc collapse, M-SD convergence, C-as-behavioural-opposition,
L defectors all lived outside the schema. The reader was marched through
Q1-Q4, then buffeted by four more sections that didn't fit.

Considered four structural alternatives:
- A. Keep structure, fix transitions (minimal)
- B. "What they say × what they do" dichotomy
- C. Thesis-led: "Eight parties, five behavioural entities"
- D. Voter-question-led: "If you are deciding in 2026..."

Picked **C**. It gives the piece an explicit thesis sentence and a
narrative climax (the Tidö collapse), demotes the four-questions scaffold
to the organising principle of one section, and ends with voter routing
instead of appending the voter section as a fifth scaffold.

New structure:
1. Hero + thesis: 8 parties on the ballot, 5 in the chamber. Name the
   five entities (Tidö as one; S alone; V+MP genuine bloc; C
   behaviourally opposition; L defectors).
2. The chamber map — establishes four of the five at party-mean level.
3. How four parties became one — the bloc-collapse climax. The L
   defectors are revealed here as the fifth entity below party averages.
4. The manifesto layer — where the eight are still eight. Q1-Q4 plus
   coherence, as subheadings inside one section.
5. The gap: stated and revealed.
6. For a 2026 voter — routing, trust register, what the data can't
   answer.

### Open questions for a fresh read

- Section 3 (the manifesto layer) is the longest. Under C it's
  supporting evidence, not the spine. May need to tighten further if a
  fresh read feels like it sags.
- The "fifth entity" framing for the L defectors is a stretch — they're
  not really a separate ballot entity, they're a within-party caucus.
  Honest about this in the prose but the naming may set the wrong
  expectation in the hero list.
- The four-questions framing in section 3 is now load-bearing locally
  but not announced up front. Reader may wonder where they came from.
